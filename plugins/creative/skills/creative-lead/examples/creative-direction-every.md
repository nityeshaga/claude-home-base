# Creative Direction: Every

**Product:** AI-native media company — publishing, tools, consulting

---

## The Vision

Every is a media company that builds AI products. It publishes long-form essays about how AI is changing creativity and work. It ships software — Cora for email, Monologue for voice dictation, Spiral for writing, Plus One for AI agents. It runs a consulting practice that helps companies like Ogilvy and Lionsgate figure out AI. And all of these things live under one roof, on one website, held together by one design language.

That's the challenge. Media companies don't usually ship products. Product companies don't usually publish essays about consciousness and the future of creative work. Consulting firms don't usually have a podcast. Every does all of it, and the website needs to make it feel like one coherent place rather than three companies sharing a domain.

The answer is to treat Every as a *place*, not a brand. Think of a bookshop that also has a small workshop in the back where they build furniture. The front room is all warm lighting, beautiful typography, and carefully arranged shelves. You come there to browse, to think, to be in the company of good ideas. But if you walk to the back, you find a workshop — tools on the wall, sawdust on the floor, people making things. Both rooms feel like they belong to the same building because they share the same care, the same taste, the same quality of attention. The bookshop doesn't feel like a front for the workshop. The workshop doesn't feel like a distraction from the bookshop. They feed each other.

That's what every.to should feel like. The editorial content is the front room. The products are the workshop. The consulting practice is the private office upstairs. You move between them naturally, and you always know you're at Every because the walls are the same warm cream color, the illustrations are the same strange collages of Greek statues and modern technology, and the voice is the same: curious, thoughtful, slightly irreverent, never pretentious.

The tagline is "What Comes Next?" — deliberately a question, not a statement. This matters for the design because it means Every should never feel finished or complete. It should feel like an ongoing exploration. Like a publication that's always working on the next issue. When you visit every.to, you should feel like there's always one more thing to discover — another essay, another product you didn't know about, a podcast episode that looks interesting. The site should reward wandering the way a good bookshop rewards browsing. You came looking for one thing and left with three.

The illustration style is the secret weapon. Lucas Crespo, Every's creative lead, calls his approach "neoclassical pop art" — classical Greco-Roman statuary mixed with modern elements and saturated color. A Greek bust wearing aviator sunglasses while piloting an airplane. Renaissance hands shaking across torn paper. A lobster rendered as a woodcut engraving. These images are generated through Midjourney and refined in Photoshop, and they are utterly unlike anything else in tech. They say: we take ideas seriously, but we don't take ourselves seriously. We read the classics, AND we think they're kind of funny.

There is zero photography on the entire site. Every image is illustration, collage, or UI mockup. This is expensive and slow and creates a signal that can't be faked: someone with real creative vision made every single image on this site. In a world where 90% of tech companies use the same stock photo of a smiling person at a laptop, Every's Greek-statue-with-sunglasses illustrations are so distinctive that you'd recognize them on a billboard from a hundred meters away.

The typography tells you which room you're in. Serif means you're reading — articles, essays, the "What Comes Next" tagline. Sans-serif means you're looking at a product or navigating. The background tells you what time of day it is in this building: warm off-white (#FAFAF7 — the color of paper, not the color of a screen) for the reading rooms, near-black for the product studios. Moving from light to dark within a single page feels like walking from the library to the workshop. Both rooms. Same building.

The products should feel like they grew out of this intellectual environment. Not market-researched into existence, but invented by people who think deeply about writing, reading, communication, and creativity. "I was writing about how AI could make email peaceful, and then I thought — why don't we just build that?" That's the energy. Every product is an essay that became software.

If a visitor remembers one thing, it should be the illustrations. The Greek statue in aviator sunglasses. The lobster woodcut. The classical hands shaking across torn paper. These are the brand. They're impossible to confuse with any other company, deeply weird in the best way, and they communicate everything about Every's identity in a single image: old meets new, serious meets playful, intellectual meets accessible.

## How We Achieve This

### The Two Typographic Voices

Signifier (Klim Type Foundry, transitional serif) handles all editorial content — article headlines, body text, the "What Comes Next" tagline. Sharp, high-contrast strokes with a literary quality. Article titles at 48px, weight 400 (not bold — the type speaks for itself). Body text at 20px with generous line height creates a reading experience that feels like a well-typeset book.

Switzer (geometric sans-serif) takes over for navigation, buttons, product marketing, UI. Clean, contemporary. The homepage hero uses Switzer at 69px, weight 500.

The Every logotype uses a custom typeface where the "V" is replaced with a calligraphic swash — a serif flourish in a clean wordmark. This glyph IS the duality: editorial meets product, tradition meets innovation.

- Article title: Signifier 48px / weight 400
- Article subtitle: Signifier 24px / weight 400
- Article body: Signifier 20px / weight 400 / line-height ~30px
- Homepage hero: Switzer 69px / weight 500
- Nav links: Switzer 18px / weight 400
- Buttons: Switzer 16px / pill-shaped (border-radius 9999px)
- Drop caps in articles use the custom "Every" font — classic magazine device

### The Color of Paper

The editorial background is #FAFAF7 — rgb(250, 250, 247). Three points warmer than pure white. This changes everything. Pure white is the color of a screen. #FAFAF7 is the color of paper — your brain reads "reflective surface" instead of "light source."

- Editorial background: #FAFAF7 (warm off-white)
- Dark sections: #111111 / #18181B (near-black, not pure black)
- Primary text: #1A1A1A (slightly softer than black)
- Muted text: rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.6) — transparency rather than fixed gray
- Accent blue: rgb(17, 123, 200) — used sparingly, a mood color not a brand color
- Subscribe button: black bg, white text, rounded pill — the only persistent CTA

### The Illustration Language

Neoclassical pop art collage: classical Greco-Roman statuary (black and white) mixed with modern objects and selective color backgrounds.

- Generated via Midjourney with simple, specific prompts and white backgrounds for isolation
- Combined/refined in Photoshop across multiple generations
- Product imagery uses the same vocabulary: woodcut-style engravings for icons (lobster, brain, lock, infinity), conference badges with classical portraits for Plus One
- Zero stock photography across the entire site

### Dark Mode as Architecture

Dark backgrounds are not a toggle — they're different rooms in the same building.

- Product pages / newsletter archive → dark (#111111). Products emerge from the night.
- Editorial content → light (#FAFAF7). Ideas live in daylight.
- Transitions happen WITHIN pages. Scrolling from cream to dark feels like walking from the library to the studio.

### Editorial Layout

- Full-bleed hero images (edge to edge, no padding)
- Narrow reading column: ~680-720px (optimal 10-12 words per line for sustained reading)
- Drop caps using custom "Every" font
- Generous line spacing — text breathes
- Navigation: hamburger menu, search, centered EVERY wordmark, "Subscribe" pill. Minimal, confident.
