# Creative Direction: Plus One

**Product:** Personal AI agent with a name, memory, personality, and infinite toolbelt

---

## The Vision

Plus One gives you an AI agent that lives in your Slack workspace. Not a chatbot you ping with questions — a persistent team member with a name, a memory of everything you've talked about, a personality, and the ability to use any tool: browse the web, write docs, send emails, manage calendars, query databases. You assign it work and it follows up, takes initiative, remembers context from last week. It works alongside your human teammates in the same Slack channels, and over time it gets better because it knows you.

The hard design problem is that AI agents are invisible. There is no product to photograph, no interface to screenshot (the interface is Slack, which already exists), no physical object to render. Every other product category has something to SHOW — a dashboard, an app, a device. Agents are pure behavior. They exist as text in chat windows and as tasks completed in the background. So the question becomes: how do you make something invisible feel present, trustworthy, and worth paying for?

The answer that most AI companies have landed on is abstraction. Glowing orbs. Neural network diagrams. Gradient blobs that pulse with "intelligence." Dark themes with neon accents and terminal-style interfaces. The message: this is powerful, futuristic, technical. The problem: it's also cold, impersonal, and indistinguishable from every other AI product page on the internet.

Plus One should go the opposite direction entirely. Instead of making agents feel like technology, make them feel like people. Not in a creepy, uncanny-valley way — in the way that a great character in a novel feels like a person. Give them names. Real names, with personality: Alfred, Zosia, Iris, Milo, Thoreau, R2C2. Give them faces — not cartoon avatars, but classical engraved portraits, the kind you'd see on a coin or a cameo. Timeless, dignified, with the weight of history behind them. Give them job titles: Operations Agent, Copywriting Agent. Give them badges.

Conference badges, specifically. On colored lanyards. The kind you get at a company offsite or a professional conference. Because a badge does something profound that no amount of gradient animation can do: it says *you belong here*. A badge is an instrument of belonging. It grants access. It says: this person has been admitted, vetted, authorized to be in this room. When the visitor sees conference badges as the hero image, the unconscious message is: these aren't tools you're buying. These are colleagues who've been cleared to join your team.

The centerpiece of the experience should be a story, not a demo. A Slack conversation — pixel-perfect, realistic, messy the way real Slack threads are — showing a team preparing for a product launch. Humans and agents working together. Iris suggests venues. Milo handles scheduling. Thoreau writes copy. The agents don't stand out as alien presences. They fit in. They're just... team members. The visitor understands what Plus One does not by reading about capabilities, but by watching them happen in context. Show, don't tell.

The feature illustrations should reject everything about corporate SaaS design. No geometric icons from Lucide or Heroicons. Instead: hand-drawn rubber stamps. Rough, imperfect, slightly eccentric. A lobster inside a wax seal. A padlock with weathered texture. A brain sketch. These feel handmade and curated — chosen by a person with taste, not generated by a design system. In a product category that's entirely abstract and digital, this commitment to physical, tactile, slightly weird imagery is a grounding force. It makes the intangible feel real and the corporate feel human.

The color palette should be almost silent. Warm off-white, white, near-black. The only real colors: an icy cyan button and a slow-cycling pastel gradient — sage green, warm gold, dusty rose, soft blue. These are not tech colors. They're botanical. They belong in a greenhouse or a watercolor studio. In a market where every AI product screams with electric purple and neon blue, this quietness says more than any gradient ever could: we're confident enough not to shout.

The competitors — Anthropic's agent tools, OpenAI's assistants, Google's Gemini agents, the dozen-plus startups — all market agents as capabilities. "Can browse the web. Can write code. Can use 100+ tools." Their design language is universally futuristic. Plus One should market agents as *colleagues*. Where competitors show dashboards, show a Slack conversation. Where competitors list capabilities, tell a story. Where competitors go dark and neon, go warm and quiet. The positioning is: our agents aren't impressive technology. They're good teammates. And that's harder to build and harder to copy.

If someone remembers one thing, it should be the badges. Those dark cards on colored lanyards with classical portraits and names like Alfred and Zosia. The moment you look at them, you understand that this company thinks about AI agents differently than everyone else. And that reframing — from tool to colleague, from feature to character — is what makes Plus One stick in your mind long after you've closed the tab.

## How We Achieve This

### Typography

Switzer variable (100-900) is the workhorse — headings at weight 500 (medium, not bold), body at 400. Medium weight positions the product as authoritative without aggressive. It's like a well-dressed person who doesn't need to announce themselves.

Signifier (Klim serif) appears exactly ONCE: the footer tagline "What Comes Next" at 42px. One serif in a sea of sans-serif creates disproportionate gravitational pull — the literary voice of Every surfacing briefly to connect Plus One to something larger.

Hierarchy uses opacity-based coloring rather than separate gray values:
- Primary: rgba(0, 0, 0, 1.0)
- Description: rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.6)
- Attribution: rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.5)
- Notes: rgba(0, 0, 0, 0.4)
- These auto-adapt to any background. Self-harmonizing palette.

Type scale:
- H1 (hero): Switzer 69px / weight 500 / line-height 1.1
- H2 (sections): Switzer 35-45px / weight 500
- Subhead: Switzer 25px / weight 400 / rgba(0,0,0,0.6)
- Body: Switzer 16px / weight 400
- Chat usernames: Switzer 13px / weight 600
- Category labels: Switzer 11px / weight 600 / uppercase / 0.08em tracking
- Footer tagline: Signifier 42px / weight 400

### Color

Extreme restraint. Almost colorless.

- Background: #FAFAF7 / rgb(250, 250, 247) — warm off-white
- White: #FFFFFF — card backgrounds
- Near-black: #1A1A1A — dark sections, chat mockup
- Footer: #000000
- Icy cyan: #C0F0FB — Subscribe button (the only "real" color element)
- Animated pastel gradient: #b8d4a3 → #e8d490 → #d4a9c4 → #a8c4d4 (sage → gold → rose → blue), cycling over 6 seconds via 400% background-size
- macOS traffic lights: rgb(236,106,94), rgb(246,194,79), rgb(98,198,85) — purely decorative but the most saturated colors on the page

### The Chat Mockup

Pixel-perfect Slack workspace inside a device frame. 4px border in #6C6C6C, border-radius 30px.

Six-layer shadow stack for genuine physical presence:
```
0 0 #0000004d,
0 9px 20px #0000004a,
0 37px 37px #00000042,
0 84px 50px #00000026,
0 149px 60px #0000000a,
0 233px 65px #00000003
```

-450px negative top margin creates dramatic overlap with the section above — physical layering.

Inside: real avatars for humans, "PLUS ONE" badge labels for agents, tool-call indicators, realistic timestamps. Content tells a narrative — team preparing product launch with humans and AI agents collaborating.

### The Illustrations

Hand-drawn rubber-stamp style inside circles. Rough, imperfect, eccentric.
- Lobster (OpenClaw) in red seal border
- Padlock with metallic/weathered texture (security)
- Brain profile (memory)
- Infinity symbol (autonomous operation)
- Hand-drawn versions of real app logos (Slack, Google, etc.)

Deliberate rejection of polished geometric icon sets. These couldn't have been generated by a design system.

### Motion

Every animation serves the narrative:
- Chat messages fade in sequentially from below (translateY 12px → 0, 0.4s ease) — pacing like a real conversation
- Chat card zoom: scales to 1.2x (1.5x on large screens) with cubic-bezier(0.16, 1, 0.3, 1) — dramatic ease-out that "lands" like something heavy settling
- Pastel gradient: 6-second infinite cycle — biological rhythmicity, the page breathes
- Marquee: horizontal Slack cards scrolling at 60s/loop, gradient-masked edges
- Role tab pill: cubic-bezier(0.34, 1.56, 0.64, 1) — bouncy overshoot (1.56), the only playful motion, inviting interaction
- Typewriter cursor: 1s blink suggesting live AI generation
